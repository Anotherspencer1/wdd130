Getting Started

Second Page: Getting Started Raised Bed, or In-Ground? Raised garden beds are growing in popularity, and for good reason. Raised garden beds are going to provide many benefits for those who choose that route. By having the soil raised up it allows the user much more control over the quality of the soil, the weeds and unwanted plants that grow there, pests that can access it, as well as providing a more ergonomic space for tending and harvesting. Some downsides to a raised garden bed involve primarily the cost of building the bed and buying soil to fill it. Either option can be very successful, and the choice is yours! Expected Costs? Gardening can end up saving you money and elevating your life by having fresh grown produce without having to stop by the store. However, getting into the hobby has some cost up-front. Obviously, cost is going to differ for every garden, but one can expect to spend money on tools, soil, fertilizer, seeds, plants, and water. Some tips to save money while gardening: Look for used tools in your local marketplaces, start composting early to get high quality soil for free, buy seeds over plants and start them inside to get a jump on the season for a fraction of the cost, and check for natural fertilizers using things you may already have. Common Pitfalls? Everyone makes mistakes while gardening, it would be wise to accept the fact that you may have some bad harvests, late blooms, diseased plants, and irritating pests. That being said, there are ways to avoid these issues and help you learn quicker. Planning is going to be essential. Try to find a local community where you can discuss plants that do well where you live, frost dates, common pests, common diseases, soil quality, etc. By studying and planning you can cut costs and save some heartache by learning from others mistakes.

Hardiness Zone Guide

Third Page: Zones and Plants An important resource for anyone starting to garden is the USDA Plant Hardiness Zone Map. The Hardiness zones are a set of zone numbers based on lowest temperatures of the area which can guide you in deciding what plants will work well in your area. We recommend that anyone who is starting to plan a garden checks what zone they are in and double check that the plants they choose work well in their zone. For example, Tomatoes love hotter weather, meaning you can plant earlier in zones 5-7, but you may need to wait until fall if you're in zones 10 and above. Every plant has different preferred conditions, which makes it essential to find out what to plant and when depending on your zone. By researching and planning this way, you can ensure that your plants are happy and healthy all season long. Greenhouses are a great resource to lengthen your season and create a micro-climate with a different zone from the rest of your yard. If you live in a zone 2 area, your season may be very short making it difficult to get a good yield of produce. However, by planting in a greenhouse you can use the heat of the sun to raise the temperatures and extend the length of your season by a month or more!
